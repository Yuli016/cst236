The Legend of Zelda Development Challenges

Needless to say, the original Legend of Zelda had plenty of issues when trying to create a game of its scale with such limited resources. A single screen could only display 256x240 pixels with a maximum sprite size of 8x16. Not only this, but there was a maximum of only 64 sprites on screen at once; with only 8 sprites being on a scan line at a time. “Sprites are two-dimensional images or animations overlaid into a scene. They are the non-static elements within a 2D game, moving independently of the background. Often used to represent player-controlled characters, props, enemy units, etc., sprites can be composed of multiple tiles or smaller sprites.” While 64 may sound like a lot, it is also worth considering that this makes up all intractable objects in the game which are not background elements. This can quickly become an issue for a game like Zelda, which has a lot of enemies in the over world and dungeons.

Aside from this issue, the concept of importing a map into a game is far different from today's standards. There is no software making it simple to sculpt a map in-game. Rather, Nintendo used a primitive yet very effective method to design the world of Zelda. They hand drew the map as shown below and would then trace the grid into the different screens traversable by the player.

Some other criticisms the NES received was the design of the game cartridge port. Nintendo designed the NES to be more appealing to Americans with the sleek design and VCR-like opening to look more iconic. The detriment to this design, however, was that the port design would slightly bend the pins of the cartridge overtime. This would lead to the NES not being able to read games as well overtime and the rise of the infamous “blow into the cartridge” technique (often leading to only worsening the conditions). However, the issue is a mostly American problem, as Nintendo’s Japanese release, The Famicom, had the port facing directly upright without bending the pins.

Ocarina of Time Development Challenges


Taking a leap from 2D into 3D presented a whole new set of challenges for Nintendo to overcome. Creating a 3D space presents a lot more creative freedoms in terms of perspective and controls; however, many early 3d games found great difficulty in the sense of distance and perspective.

Another issue that challenged development was the creation of polygonal walls surrounding the locations in Ocarina of Time. “ [According] to dungeon designer Eiji Onozuka [….] Link had a variety of impressive maneuvers but unfortunately many of his moves would cause him to "clip through walls" (such as the Hookshot). According to Onozuka, the mappers of Ocarina of Time, of which there were three, had the most trouble of the entire game, and he said that there would be occasions where they would actually cry.”


On the more technical side, limitations of the N64 would come to pose problems for graphical fidelity. The 150,000 per second polygon load of the N64 sounds impressive at first. Despite this, the characters themselves already take up roughly 400 triangles; this shows that the N64 uses every single one of those polys to their full potential. Texturing would also pose a difficult problem for modelling inside Zelda; many characters would have to recycle the same UV maps for different parts of the model to make it work. Sheik is an excellent example of ingenuity from Nintendo; the texture used on her arms is the same on the waist and legs.

Skyward Sword was a game that faced many challenges during development. The game spent five years in production with two of them being experimental projects which ultimately failed. Alongside this long development time, the game suffered from franchise fatigue and a loss of identity. With mixed reception from Twilight Princess, the prior Legend of Zelda game, Nintendo felt the need to make a game which had meaning and didn't feel like an obligatory release. Having a new game director didn't help the cause either as the prior lead stepped down to a project manager who would step in at time of need. This game experimented with a vastly open world which was challenging to give structure. However, some ideas from other games began to seep into Skyward Swords identity and help the game move along.


Around this time, the concept of Link flying around on a red bird was introduced – a method of travelling between discrete areas that echoed The Wind Waker’s boat excursions. Interestingly, planner Daiki Iwamoto mentions in the same Iwata Asks interview that Skyward Sword’s structure was inspired by the course selection screen in Super Mario, where the Italian hero could trot around and hop into whichever area the player chose. In fact, Aonuma suggests that this quite new, compartmentalised design approach for Skyward Sword may have been its saving grace.


With a game that covers the land and sky, Nintendo needed to formulate a way to keep it simple but also engaging. By limiting the scale via the holes in the sky, the player feels like they have a choice on where to go despite only being able to access the floor below via specific locations.


“I think we were able to keep such a big project together because the game world this time is structurally simple,” Aonuma said. “We talk about all these ‘dense’ places, but structure-wise there are only four – forest, volcano, desert and sky.

Breath of the Wild was, in many ways, a direct improvement over Skyward Sword. It took the open world experience and enhanced it into a fully playable world using a newer generation of consoles. The game had a dual release on both the Wii U and Switch, which resulted in a need to overhaul some designs of the game to make it work for both consoles. “So that idea of having a large open connected world was in my mind as soon as Skyward Sword was finished, really. And then the Wii U hardware made realising that idea a possibility.” “[Regarding] the Nintendo Switch version, it was spring last year when we made a firm decision to also release on that platform. Obviously that required some adjustments to the development process and changes to be made, and to continue developing the Wii U version alongside Nintendo Switch, that was spring last year.” “Of course on Wii U you have two screens - the main screen, and one on the GamePad - but the Nintendo Switch has one screen. That was a major change, but we achieved it a lot more easily and quickly than we expected.”


Another technical marvel for the game was its ability to use a physics engine to solve puzzles, this new form of interaction with the game world was never before seen in the Zelda franchise. “Another example of a challenge we faced was the physics engine. We wanted a consistent physics engine throughout the world that worked in a logical and realistic way.” “The way the physics engine underpins everything in the world really offers up a lot of new possibilities. For instance, in Breath of the Wild you might have a puzzle where making use of the physics, there'll be various ways you can solve that puzzle.”
